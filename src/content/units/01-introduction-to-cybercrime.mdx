## What is cybercrime?

**Cybercrime is any illegal activity in which a computer, network, or networked device is used as a _target_, a _weapon_, or an _instrument_ to commit an offence.** It may be aimed at a person, a company, or a government system, usually to steal data, make money illegally, cause damage, or disturb normal digital activity.

### Why it matters

Almost everything — banking, study, work, communication — now runs on digital devices, so cybercrime directly threatens **privacy, money, reputation, and even national security**. It is hard to detect because attackers can hide their identity and operate from anywhere in the world.

### The three categories (the "triad" of cybercrime)

1. **Against individuals** — cyberstalking, identity theft, online fraud.
2. **Against property** — unauthorized access, software piracy, intellectual-property theft.
3. **Against government / organisations** — cyberterrorism, attacks on critical infrastructure.

### What makes cybercrime different from ordinary crime

- **No geographical boundary** — an attacker in one country can hit a victim in another.
- **High speed and anonymity** — attacks happen in seconds and the criminal stays hidden.
- **Leaves digital footprints (logs)** that require specialised **digital forensics** to investigate.
- **Governed by special laws** — e.g. the **IT Act** in India, the **Computer Misuse Act** in the UK, and international frameworks like the **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime**.

## Forgery (cyber forgery)

**Cyber forgery is altering, fabricating, or counterfeiting digital documents, signatures, or electronic records with the intent to deceive, defraud, or gain an unfair advantage.** There is no pen — the criminal manipulates *data*.

### Common forms

- **Email spoofing** — modifying email headers so a message looks like it came from a trusted sender (used heavily in phishing).
- **Digital-signature forgery** — compromising or mimicking a user's private cryptographic key to sign fraudulent transactions.
- Altering electronic medical records, financial statements, invoices, or academic certificates.

### Impact and legality

Forgery destroys the security pillar of **Integrity** (trust that data is genuine and unchanged). Because digital records are accepted in business, education, and government, a forged electronic document can cause financial fraud and legal confusion. Penalties are equal to or harsher than traditional forgery.

## Hacking

**Hacking is identifying and exploiting weaknesses in a computer system or network to gain unauthorized access to data or resources.** In law, the deciding factors are **intent** and **authorization**.

### The three "hats"

- **Black hat** — malicious attackers who break security for personal gain, destruction, or espionage (**illegal**).
- **White hat** — ethical hackers hired to legally find and fix security gaps (**legal and essential for defence**).
- **Grey hat** — find weaknesses without permission but without malicious intent, often asking for a fee to fix them (**legally ambiguous**).

<HackerHats />

### Typical process of a hack

1. **Reconnaissance** — gather information.
2. **Scanning** — look for vulnerabilities.
3. **Gaining access**.
4. **Maintaining access**.
5. **Clearing tracks**.

<Callout type="tip">
Not every skilled technical act is a crime. **Hacking becomes criminal when access is _unauthorized_ or causes harm** — it violates a system's **Confidentiality** and **Availability**.
</Callout>

## Software piracy

**Software piracy is the unauthorized copying, reproduction, distribution, or commercial use of copyrighted software.** Software is protected by copyright, so unlicensed use violates **Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)**.

### Types of piracy

- **Softlifting** — sharing one program with friends or installing a single licence on many computers.
- **Internet piracy** — uploading or downloading cracked software from torrents or shady sites.
- **Hard-disk loading** — dealers pre-installing counterfeit software on hardware to attract buyers.

### Why it is harmful

- Deprives developers of revenue and slows innovation.
- "Cracked" activators frequently hide **malware**.
- Leads to civil liabilities (damages) and criminal penalties for large-scale distributors.

## Computer network intrusion

**Network intrusion is any unauthorized activity or breach on a network that disrupts performance, compromises security, or steals data.** It is a deliberate bypass of firewalls and security policies — essentially **digital trespassing**.

### How intrusion happens

- Weak passwords and poor network settings.
- Phishing emails and malware/spyware.
- **Port scanning & sniffing** to find open entry points.
- Exploiting unpatched software vulnerabilities.
- Flooding the network with traffic (**DoS**) to crash it.

### Detection and prevention

Organisations deploy **Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)** and **Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS)** to watch traffic for suspicious patterns or known attack signatures. Because networks hold huge amounts of user data, a breach often triggers **mandatory data-breach notification laws** and heavy fines if negligence is proven.

<Callout type="exam">
**One-line answers**

- **Cybercrime:** illegal activity using a computer/network as target, weapon, or instrument.
- **Forgery:** making or using fake digital documents/records to deceive (breaks Integrity).
- **Hacking:** unauthorized access to a system or data.
- **Software piracy:** unlawful copying or use of copyrighted software (breaks IPR).
- **Network intrusion:** unauthorized entry into a network — digital trespassing.
