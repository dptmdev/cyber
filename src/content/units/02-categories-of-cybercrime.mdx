## How criminals plan attacks

A common misunderstanding is that cyberattacks are random or spur-of-the-moment. In reality, **serious cybercriminals plan attacks carefully in stages.** This structured plan is often described using the **Cyber Kill Chain** model, which breaks an attack into the sequence of steps an attacker must complete to succeed. Understanding it is valuable because **breaking the chain at any single stage stops the whole attack** — this is the basis of "defence in depth".

### The stages of planning, in detail

1. **Reconnaissance (information gathering)** — the attacker first selects a target and learns everything possible about it.
   - *Passive reconnaissance* uses publicly available information without touching the target — company websites, LinkedIn, social media, news, public records.
   - *Active reconnaissance* directly probes the target, e.g. scanning its network for open ports and live services.
2. **Weaponization & staging** — the attacker pairs an **exploit** (a way to abuse a specific vulnerability) with a **payload** (the malicious code that does the damage, such as ransomware or spyware), and sets up infrastructure like fake domains or command-and-control (C2) servers.
3. **Delivery** — the weapon is sent to the victim, commonly through a **phishing email**, a malicious link, or an infected USB drive.
4. **Exploitation** — the victim triggers the exploit (e.g. opens the attachment), giving the attacker an initial foothold.
5. **Installation / maintaining access** — the attacker installs backdoors so they can return even if the original hole is closed.
6. **Command & control and actions** — the attacker remotely controls the system, then achieves their goal: stealing data, encrypting files, or spreading further.
7. **Covering tracks** — logs are deleted to delay or prevent detection.

### Why this matters legally and defensively

Knowing the stages lets defenders place controls at every step (email filters, network monitoring, least-privilege access, backups). Under cyber law, **even the preparatory acts** — writing malware, building tools, or buying attack infrastructure — are treated as offences in many jurisdictions, even if the final attack is intercepted before it succeeds.

## Passive attacks

**A passive attack is one in which the attacker secretly intercepts, monitors, or copies data passing through a system, without changing the data or disrupting the system in any way.** The aim is purely to **steal information** — to learn something the attacker is not supposed to know.

### How a passive attack works

Because nothing is altered and no service is interrupted, the system continues to function perfectly normally. The victim usually has **no idea** the attack is happening, which is what makes passive attacks **extremely difficult to detect**.

### Common types

1. **Eavesdropping / sniffing** — capturing and reading data packets as they travel across a network, especially **unencrypted** traffic on a LAN or public Wi-Fi. Tools called packet sniffers can read usernames, passwords, and messages sent "in the clear".
2. **Traffic analysis** — even when the data itself is **encrypted** and cannot be read, the attacker studies the *pattern* of communication: who is talking to whom, how often, message sizes, and timing. From this they can deduce identities, relationships, and activities.

### Security impact and prevention

Passive attacks attack **Confidentiality** — the "C" of the CIA triad. Since detection is nearly impossible, the main defence is **prevention through strong encryption** (HTTPS, TLS, VPNs), which makes any intercepted data unreadable, plus avoiding untrusted public networks.

## Active attacks

**An active attack is a deliberate attempt to alter system resources, modify or destroy data, or disrupt the normal operation of a system or network.** Unlike a passive attack, the attacker *interacts aggressively* with the system, so the attack **changes the state of the system and leaves a visible footprint** that can later be detected in logs.

### Common types

1. **Masquerading (spoofing)** — the attacker pretends to be a legitimate user or system to gain access and privileges they should not have.
2. **Replay** — capturing a valid data transmission (e.g. a login token) and re-sending it later to gain access.
3. **Modification of messages** — intercepting genuine data and altering it before it reaches the receiver, e.g. changing the account number in a money transfer.
4. **Denial of Service (DoS)** — overwhelming a system with junk traffic so it crashes and becomes unavailable to genuine users.

### Security impact

Active attacks break **Integrity** (data is changed) and **Availability** (service is disrupted). They cause immediate, severe financial and operational damage. They are *easier to detect* than passive attacks but *harder to prevent completely*, and they carry the heaviest criminal penalties because of their destructive nature.

## Cyberstalking

**Cyberstalking is the repeated and deliberate use of the internet, social media, email, or other electronic communication to harass, threaten, intimidate, or monitor a specific victim.** It is the online form of stalking, and it is defined by its **repetitive, persistent, and obsessive** nature — a single rude message is not cyberstalking; a sustained campaign is.

### Methods used by cyberstalkers

- Sending repeated threatening or abusive emails, messages, and social-media comments.
- **Doxxing** — publicly releasing the victim's private information (home address, phone number, workplace) to expose them and invite others to harass them.
- Creating **fake accounts** to impersonate, monitor, or trap the victim.
- Using **spyware / stalkerware** or unauthorized **GPS tracking** to secretly monitor the victim's location, calls, and online activity.
- Spreading false or private information to damage the victim's reputation.

### Effects on the victim

Cyberstalking causes serious **psychological harm** — fear, anxiety, stress, and loss of privacy. Because the harassment follows the victim everywhere through their devices, it can feel inescapable, and it frequently **escalates into real-world physical danger**.

### Legal seriousness

Modern cyber law treats cyberstalking as a **distinct criminal offence**, often punishable with imprisonment and restraining orders. Its recognition marks an important shift: cybercrime is no longer seen as only economic fraud, but also as a genuine **threat to human safety**.

<Callout type="exam">
**One-line answers**

- **Attack planning:** a staged process (recon → weaponize → deliver → exploit → install → control → cover tracks) — the cyber kill chain.
- **Passive attack:** secretly observing/copying data without changing it (breaks Confidentiality; hard to detect).
- **Active attack:** altering, faking, or disrupting data/services (breaks Integrity & Availability; leaves a trace).
- **Cyberstalking:** repeated, deliberate online harassment, threats, or monitoring of a victim.
