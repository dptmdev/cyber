## How criminals plan attacks

Cybercriminals rarely act randomly. They follow a structured, multi-stage plan — often called the **Cyber Kill Chain** — to maximise success and avoid detection.

<AttackLifecycle />

### The planning stages

1. **Reconnaissance (information gathering)** — choose a target and collect intelligence. *Passive* research uses public records, LinkedIn, and social media; *active* research scans the target's network for open ports.
2. **Weaponization & staging** — pair an **exploit** (a software weakness) with a **payload** (malware), and set up infrastructure such as fake domains or command-and-control servers.
3. **Delivery & exploitation** — send the weapon (phishing email, corrupted USB) and run it to gain an initial foothold.
4. **Exploit / gain access**, then **maintain access** and **cover tracks**.

### Why study this?

Knowing *how* attacks are planned lets defenders use **defence-in-depth** — breaking the chain at any single stage stops the attack. Legally, in many places **even planning or building attack tools** (like writing ransomware) is an offence, even if the attack is stopped before it runs.

## Passive attacks

**A passive attack intercepts or monitors data without altering it.** The goal is to steal information, so the system keeps working normally — which makes passive attacks **very hard to detect**.

### Common types

1. **Eavesdropping / sniffing** — capturing and reading unencrypted packets on a LAN or public Wi-Fi.
2. **Traffic analysis** — even when data is encrypted, watching the *frequency, size, and destination* of messages to deduce who is talking to whom.

### Security impact

Passive attacks break **Confidentiality** (the *C* in the CIA triad). Since detection is nearly impossible, the main defence is **prevention through strong encryption** (HTTPS, VPNs) so intercepted data is unreadable.

## Active attacks

**An active attack alters system resources, modifies data, or disrupts normal operation.** Unlike passive attacks, it changes the system's state, so it **leaves a visible footprint**.

### Common types

1. **Masquerading (spoofing)** — pretending to be a legitimate user/system to gain privileges.
2. **Modification of messages** — intercepting and altering data in transit (e.g. changing an account number in a wire transfer).
3. **Denial of Service (DoS)** — flooding a system with junk traffic so it crashes and becomes unavailable.

### Security impact

Active attacks break **Integrity** and **Availability**. They are easier to detect (logs show the change) but cause immediate, severe damage — so they carry the heaviest criminal penalties.

## Cyberstalking

**Cyberstalking is repeatedly using the internet, social media, email, or other digital tools to harass, threaten, monitor, or intimidate a victim.** It is defined by its **repetitive and obsessive** nature.

### Common behaviour

- Repeated threatening emails, texts, or spammed social-media comments.
- **Doxxing** — publicly releasing a victim's private information (home address, phone number) to invite others to harass them.
- Using **spyware / stalkerware** or unauthorized GPS tracking to monitor a victim's location and online activity.
- Creating fake accounts to harass or impersonate.

### Why it is serious

Cyberstalking violates the right to **privacy and safety** and can escalate into **real-world violence**. Modern cyber law treats it as a distinct offence, often punishable with jail time and restraining orders — shifting cybercrime from "just fraud" to a threat to physical human safety.

<Callout type="exam">
**One-line answers**

- **Attack planning:** a staged process (recon → weaponize → deliver → exploit → control) — the cyber kill chain.
- **Passive attack:** secretly observing data without changing it (breaks Confidentiality).
- **Active attack:** altering, faking, or disrupting data/services (breaks Integrity & Availability).
- **Cyberstalking:** repeated online harassment, threats, or monitoring of a victim.
