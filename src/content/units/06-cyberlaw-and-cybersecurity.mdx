**Cybercrime** is illegal activity done with computers/networks; **cybersecurity** is protecting systems, networks, and data from those attacks. The two are linked — cybersecurity exists to stop or reduce cybercrime.

## Legal aspects of cybercrime

Cybercrime is not only a *technical* problem — it's a *legal* one. Traditional penal codes were written for physical assets, so cyber law provides a framework for **intangible digital property, electronic records, and borderless offences**.

### Why cyber law is needed

- To define what is illegal online.
- To punish offenders and protect victims and data.
- To regulate digital transactions and e-governance.
- To set rules for collecting and using **electronic evidence**.

### Key legal challenges

1. **Jurisdiction** — crimes are often cross-border (a hacker in Europe hitting a target in Asia), so it's unclear which country's police can investigate.
2. **Admissibility of evidence** — digital data is volatile and easily altered, so the law sets strict **chain-of-custody** standards for logs and drives to stand up in court.

International frameworks like the **Budapest Convention** let member countries cooperate and share digital forensics across borders.

## Indian cyber laws

India uses a **dual approach**:

1. **The IT Act (special law)** — handles electronic data, digital signatures, hacking, and platform regulation.
2. **The IPC / BNS (general criminal law)** — traditional criminal law applied alongside cyber law (e.g. online forgery uses IPC provisions plus electronic-proof rules).

**Key agencies:** **CERT-In** (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team), the national watchdog for cyber threats, and dedicated **Cyber Police Stations** across states.

## The Information Technology Act, 2000

**The IT Act, 2000 is India's primary law on cybercrime, e-commerce, and digital signatures.** It gives **legal recognition to electronic records, digital signatures, and e-governance**, and defines digital offences and penalties.

### Frequently-tested sections

| Section | What it covers |
| :-- | :-- |
| **43** | Penalty for damaging a computer/introducing viruses — compensation up to **₹1 crore** |
| **66** | Hacking / computer-related fraud — up to **3 years** imprisonment |
| **66C & 66D** | Identity theft and cheating by personation online |
| **66F** | **Cyber terrorism** (threatening India's sovereignty/security) — up to **life imprisonment** |
| **35** | Authorises the **Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA)** to oversee digital-certificate issuers |

### Notable amendments

- The **2008 Amendment** expanded privacy protections and added many of the above sections.
- **Section 66A** (criminalising "offensive" messages) was **struck down** by the Supreme Court in **_Shreya Singhal v. Union of India_ (2015)** for violating free speech.

<Callout type="tip">
Remember the headline numbers: **43 → ₹1 crore damage**, **66 → 3 years hacking**, **66F → life for cyber terrorism**, and the **Shreya Singhal (2015)** case that killed Section 66A.
</Callout>

## Public Key Certificate (PKC / Digital Signature Certificate)

**A Public Key Certificate is an electronic credential, issued by a trusted third party, that mathematically binds a public key to the verified identity of its holder.** It lets people trust that a public key really belongs to the claimed person or organisation.

### How it works (asymmetric cryptography)

A pair of keys is used:

- **Private key** — kept secret; used to **sign** documents.
- **Public key** — shared openly; used by others to **verify** that signature.

The certificate is issued and vouched for by a **Certifying Authority (CA)**.

### What a certificate contains

Owner's name · the public key · expiry date · Certifying Authority details · the CA's digital signature.

### Legal standing (under the IT Act)

- **Section 35** lets the **Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA)** license the companies that issue certificates.
- A document signed with a valid Digital Signature Certificate has the **same legal validity as a handwritten signature** in an Indian court.

<Callout type="tip">
**Example:** a business owner files company documents on the MCA portal using a **DSC USB token**; the system validates it with the CA, proving identity without any physical meeting.
</Callout>
