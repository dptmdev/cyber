It helps to fix two terms first. **Cybercrime** is illegal activity carried out using computers and networks. **Cybersecurity** is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and data from those attacks. The two are two sides of the same coin — cybersecurity exists to *prevent, detect, and respond to* cybercrime, and the **law** provides the framework to *punish* it and to make digital evidence usable in court.

## Legal aspects of cybercrime and cybersecurity

Cybercrime is not only a technical problem; it is fundamentally a **legal** one. Traditional penal codes were written for *physical* assets — they do not map neatly onto intangible digital property, electronic records, or borderless offences. Cyber law fills this gap.

### Why cyber law is needed

- To clearly **define** which online acts are illegal.
- To **punish** offenders and **protect** victims and their data.
- To give **legal recognition** to electronic records, contracts, and digital signatures, so e-commerce and e-governance can function.
- To set rules for **collecting, preserving, and presenting electronic evidence**.

### Key legal challenges

Two challenges come up again and again and make excellent answer points:

1. **Jurisdiction.** Cybercrimes are typically **cross-border** — a hacker in one country attacking a server in another. It becomes unclear *which* country's police have the authority to investigate and *which* country's laws apply.
2. **Admissibility of evidence.** Digital data is **volatile** and easily altered or deleted. The law therefore demands a strict **chain of custody** — documented proof of how logs, hard drives, and files were collected and stored — so the evidence can stand up in court.

International cooperation frameworks such as the **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime** let member countries share digital forensics and coordinate across borders.

## Indian cyber laws

India tackles cybercrime through a **dual approach** — a special law plus the general criminal law working together:

1. **The IT Act (special law)** — deals with electronic records, digital signatures, hacking, data, and platform/intermediary regulation.
2. **The IPC / BNS (general criminal law)** — traditional criminal provisions applied alongside cyber law; for example, **online forgery or cheating** is prosecuted using IPC/BNS provisions *plus* the electronic-evidence rules of the IT Act.

**Key agencies and bodies:**
- **CERT-In (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team)** — the national nodal agency that responds to cyber incidents and issues security advisories.
- **Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA)** — regulates the issuers of digital certificates.
- Dedicated **Cyber Police Stations** and **cybercrime reporting portals** across states.

## The Information Technology Act, 2000

**The IT Act, 2000 is India's primary cyber law.** It was enacted to give **legal recognition to electronic transactions, electronic records, and digital signatures**, to enable e-governance, and to define cyber offences along with their penalties. It is based on the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce.

### Frequently-tested sections

| Section | What it covers |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Sec 43** | Penalty for damaging a computer, unauthorized access, or introducing a virus — compensation up to **₹1 crore** |
| **Sec 66** | Computer-related offences / hacking and fraud — up to **3 years** imprisonment and/or fine |
| **Sec 66C** | **Identity theft** (fraudulent use of another's password, digital signature, etc.) |
| **Sec 66D** | **Cheating by personation** using a computer resource (online impersonation fraud) |
| **Sec 66F** | **Cyber terrorism** — threatening India's unity, integrity, or security — up to **life imprisonment** |
| **Sec 67** | Publishing or transmitting **obscene material** in electronic form |
| **Sec 35** | Authorises a **Certifying Authority** to issue Digital Signature Certificates (overseen by the CCA) |

### Notable amendments and case law

- The **2008 Amendment** significantly expanded the Act — adding data-protection duties, cyber-terrorism (66F), and identity-related offences (66C/66D).
- **Section 66A**, which criminalised sending "offensive" messages, was **struck down by the Supreme Court** in **_Shreya Singhal v. Union of India_ (2015)** for being vague and violating the constitutional right to **free speech** — a landmark case worth naming.

Remember the headline numbers: **43 → ₹1 crore** (damage), **66 → 3 years** (hacking), **66C/66D → identity theft & impersonation**, **66F → life** (cyber terrorism), and **Shreya Singhal (2015)** struck down **66A**.
</Callout>

## Public Key Certificate (Digital Signature Certificate)

**A Public Key Certificate (PKC), also called a Digital Signature Certificate (DSC), is an electronic document issued by a trusted third party that mathematically binds a public key to the verified identity of its holder.** In short, it is the digital equivalent of an identity card for a cryptographic key — it lets others trust that a given public key really belongs to the named person or organisation.

### Background — asymmetric (public-key) cryptography

A PKC works on **asymmetric cryptography**, which uses a **pair of keys**:

- a **private key**, kept secret by the owner and used to **sign** documents, and
- a **public key**, shared openly and used by others to **verify** that signature.

What one key locks, only the other can unlock. The signer keeps the private key; anyone can verify with the public key. (Contrast this with **symmetric** cryptography, which uses a single shared secret key for both encryption and decryption.)

The remaining problem is trust: how do you know a public key truly belongs to the claimed person? That is solved by a **Certifying Authority (CA)** — a trusted body that verifies identity and issues a certificate vouching for the key.

### What a certificate contains

The owner's **name**, their **public key**, a **validity/expiry date**, the **issuing Certifying Authority's details**, a unique serial number, and the **CA's own digital signature** that makes it tamper-proof.

### Uses

Securing websites (HTTPS/SSL), signing documents and emails, encrypted communication, e-commerce and online banking, and proving identity for e-governance.

### Legal standing under the IT Act

- **Section 35** authorises a licensed **Certifying Authority** (regulated by the **Controller of Certifying Authorities**) to issue these certificates to citizens.
- A document signed with a valid, certified digital signature has the **same legal validity as a traditional handwritten signature** on paper in an Indian court.

<Callout type="tip">
**Example:** a business owner files company documents on the Ministry of Corporate Affairs portal using a **DSC USB token**. The system validates the certificate with the CA, proving the owner's identity online without any physical meeting.
</Callout>
